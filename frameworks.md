# Framework library

Each entry below is a structural skeleton, not a script. Rewrite every line in the sender's actual voice — the placeholders in `{{ }}` mark what needs real, researched specifics, not filler. Never stack two frameworks into one email.

Placeholders come in two kinds, and they get different treatment:

- **Identity placeholders** — `{{name}}`, `{{their company}}`, `{{sender name}}`, and similar — must always resolve to something real in the final draft. Never leave the literal `{{name}}` in a sendable email. If the recipient's name specifically isn't known, don't guess one — see SKILL.md Step 4 for the role-neutral opener to use instead.
- **Proof-point placeholders** — `{{comparable company}}`, `{{specific result}}`, `{{before metric}} to {{after metric}}`, and similar — require *real* data: something the sender supplied, or something found and verified during Step 2 research. If no real comparable company or result exists, don't invent one to fill the slot. A fabricated case study is a worse failure than a shorter email: drop the clause containing the placeholder, rewrite the surrounding sentence so it reads naturally without it, and tell the sender in Step 6 what real proof point would strengthen the draft if they're able to supply one later.

For each framework: when to reach for it, why it actually works (the human tendency it draws on — this is the part that doesn't expire), and a skeleton.

---

## 1. Trigger-Event

**When:** Something genuinely newsworthy just happened to the recipient or their company — funding, a launch, an award, a new role, an expansion.

**Why it works:** Timing signals relevance. A message that arrives right after a relevant event reads as responsive to *them*, not as part of a batch sent on a Tuesday because a list existed. This only works if the trigger is real and specifically theirs — a vague "hope business is going well" defeats the entire point.

**Skeleton:**
```
Subject: Congrats on {{specific trigger}}

Hi {{name}},

Saw the news about {{specific trigger, one sentence, in your own words}} — congrats.

Companies at this stage usually run into {{one relevant challenge tied to the trigger}}. We helped {{a comparable company}} with exactly that, and they saw {{specific result}}.

Worth a quick call to see if it's relevant for you too? {{low-friction time suggestion or scheduling link}}

{{sender name}}
```

## 2. Problem-Agitate-Solve (PAS)

**When:** The recipient almost certainly already feels a known pain point in their role or industry — you don't need to introduce the problem, just name it precisely.

**Why it works:** People act to escape discomfort more readily than to pursue a gain. Naming a real, specific frustration accurately ("yes, that's exactly it") builds instant credibility before you've said anything about your product.

**Skeleton:**
```
Subject: {{specific pain point, stated plainly}}

Hi {{name}},

{{One sentence naming a specific, real frustration people in their role deal with — not a generic "struggling to keep up."}}

Left unaddressed, that usually costs teams {{concrete cost — time, money, churn, risk}}.

We built {{product}} specifically to fix this for {{who}}, and {{comparable company}} used it to {{specific result}}.

Have 15 minutes this week to see if it'd help {{their company}} too?

{{sender name}}
```

## 3. Attention-Interest-Desire-Action (AIDA)

**When:** The recipient is a strong fit for the ideal customer profile and you can be specific about what they want, not just what they struggle with.

**Why it works:** It's a map of how attention actually moves — from noticing, to caring, to wanting, to acting. Skipping a stage (jumping from attention straight to a hard ask) is usually why templated emails feel pushy.

**Skeleton:**
```
Subject: {{attention-grabbing, specific benefit or question}}

Hi {{name}},

{{One line that earns attention — a real number, a sharp question, a specific observation about them.}}

{{Company}} helps teams like {{recipient's company}} {{core benefit}}. We do it by:
- {{concrete capability 1}}
- {{concrete capability 2}}
- {{concrete capability 3}}

Worth 15 minutes to see if it's a fit? {{time suggestion or link}}

{{sender name}}
```

---

## 4. Before-After-Bridge (BAB)

**When:** The value is easiest to grasp as a contrast — life with the current friction vs. life without it.

**Why it works:** People are better at evaluating a vivid before/after contrast than an abstract feature list. Painting the "after" does the selling; the product is just the bridge to get there.

**Skeleton:**
```
Subject: {{the "after" state, stated as a benefit}}

Hi {{name}},

Right now, teams like yours are probably dealing with {{specific current friction}}.

With {{product}}, that turns into {{specific improved state}} — {{comparable company}} went from {{before metric}} to {{after metric}}.

Want 15 minutes to see how the bridge works for {{their company}} specifically?

{{sender name}}
```

---

## 5. Praise-Picture-Push (PPP)

**When:** There's a genuine, specific, verifiable achievement to acknowledge — a talk, an award, a launch, a promotion.

**Why it works:** Sincere, specific recognition is disarming in a way generic flattery isn't — the specificity is what makes it read as true rather than as a technique. This only holds up if the compliment is accurate and would still be true if the product didn't exist.

**Skeleton:**
```
Subject: Congrats on {{specific achievement}}

Hi {{name}},

Congrats on {{specific, verifiable achievement}} — {{one genuine sentence on why that's notable}}.

Around moments like this, teams often find {{relevant challenge tied to the achievement — e.g., scaling, hiring, visibility}} becomes the next priority. If that's true for you, I'd love to show you how {{product}} helps with exactly that.

Worth 15 minutes next week?

{{sender name}}
```

## 6. Quick-Question / Three-Sentence Format

**When:** A time-strapped decision-maker, or when you want the highest possible reply rate on the lowest possible reading investment.

**Why it works:** Brevity itself is a form of respect that busy people register — a short email signals you value their time, which makes them more willing to spend a little of it on you. The three parts (who/what, why-it-matters, ask) are the entire persuasion; nothing else is load-bearing.

**Skeleton:**
```
Subject: Quick question

Hi {{name}},

{{One sentence: who you are / what you do, in plain terms.}}

{{One sentence: the specific benefit relevant to them, ideally with a number.}}

{{One sentence: the single ask — e.g., "Worth 10 minutes this week?" or "Can you point me to the right person for this?"}}

{{sender name}}
```

## 7. Right-Person Redirect

**When:** You're not confident this is the correct contact and don't want to waste their time or yours pitching the wrong person.

**Why it works:** Asking someone to redirect you is a much smaller favor than asking them to buy something, so it clears a lower bar for a reply — and a busy person would usually rather forward an email than write a decline.

**Skeleton:**
```
Subject: Quick redirect?

Hi {{name}},

We help {{target function/role}} teams {{one-sentence core value}}, and I think that might matter to whoever owns {{specific responsibility}} at {{their company}}.

Is that you, or could you point me to the right person?

Thanks either way,
{{sender name}}
```

---

## 8. Introduction Request

**When:** There's no warm path in at all, but a plausible connector exists (a mutual contact, a shared network, a non-competing peer).

**Why it works:** People are generally willing to make low-cost social connections when the ask is specific and the reason is clear — it's a much smaller commitment than being asked to evaluate a product themselves.

**Skeleton:**
```
Subject: Could you make an introduction?

Hi {{name}},

I'm hoping to connect with {{target person}} at {{target company}} about {{specific, non-generic reason}}.

Would you be comfortable making that introduction? Happy to send over a short blurb you can forward if that's easier.

Thanks for considering it,
{{sender name}}
```

---

## 9. Competitor-Mention

**When:** You know, from research, that the recipient currently uses a specific competing product or approach.

**Why it works:** People pay closer attention to a comparison against something they already use than to a pitch about a category they haven't considered — it's concrete rather than hypothetical.

**Skeleton:**
```
Subject: {{their current tool}} vs. {{product}}

Hi {{name}},

Noticed {{their company}} uses {{competitor/current approach}} — how's that been working?

{{Product}} does the same core job, but {{one specific, honest differentiator}}. Curious whether that gap matters enough to be worth a look.

Open to a quick call to compare notes?

{{sender name}}
```

---

## 10. Data-Backed / Social-Proof-Led

**When:** A long sales cycle, an enterprise buyer, or a high price point where credibility needs to be established fast, before anything else.

**Why it works:** Concrete evidence from comparable companies substitutes for trust that hasn't been built yet through a relationship — it answers "why should I believe this claim" before the reader has to ask it.

**Skeleton:**
```
Subject: {{specific metric}} for companies like {{their company}}

Hi {{name}},

We've helped companies like {{2-3 relevant, comparable names}} achieve {{specific, verifiable result}}.

Given {{one detail about their situation from research}}, I think there's a similar opportunity at {{their company}}.

Worth 15 minutes to walk through how it applied for them?

{{sender name}}
```

---

## 11. Value-Give / No-Ask

**When:** You want to open a relationship with something genuinely useful and no immediate ask — good for cold, high-value targets where trust needs to come first.

**Why it works:** Reciprocity is one of the more reliable levers in human behavior — providing real, unconditional value first makes a future ask land differently than an opener that asks for something up front.

**Skeleton:**
```
Subject: Thought this might be useful

Hi {{name}},

Came across {{their content/announcement/situation}} and it reminded me of {{a genuinely relevant resource — article, benchmark, tool}}.

{{One sentence on why it's specifically relevant to them, not generic.}}

No agenda here — happy to share more if useful, or just curious what you think.

{{sender name}}
```

---

## 12. Warm-Signal Follow-Up

**When:** The recipient has already taken some action — visited a site, downloaded something, engaged with content — so this isn't fully cold.

**Why it works:** Referencing a real action they took (accurately, not presumptuously) proves this isn't a mass blast, and lets you skip straight past the "who is this and why are they emailing me" hurdle.

**Skeleton:**
```
Subject: Following up on {{specific action}}

Hi {{name}},

Saw you {{specific action — downloaded X, visited Y page}}. Were you looking into {{relevant topic}}, or something more specific like {{a narrower guess}}?

Happy to share {{a directly relevant resource}}, or if it's more useful, a quick call to point you in the right direction.

{{sender name}}
```

---

## Choosing between close calls

- **Trigger-Event vs. Data-Backed:** use Trigger-Event when timing itself is the hook; use Data-Backed when credibility, not timing, is the obstacle.
- **PAS vs. AIDA:** use PAS when the pain point alone is enough to earn interest; use AIDA when you also need to show the mechanism, not just name the problem.
- **Quick-Question vs. everything else:** when in doubt, or when the recipient's seniority/time-scarcity is the dominant constraint, defaulting to Quick-Question is rarely wrong — it's the lowest-risk option in the library.
